Cost of Care · The Landscape

The measurement problem

Every primer before this one counts what disease costs. That is the weakest form of analysis available. There is a far stronger question — what does it cost to buy a year of healthy life? — and health economics knows exactly how to answer it. The trouble is where it has been aiming.

Three levels of analysis

Not all cost analysis is the same, and the differences are not academic. They determine what a number can actually tell you, and what it can be used to decide.

1
Cost of illness

What a disease costs. Diabetes is $413 billion a year. Back pain is the leading cause of disability on Earth. This is the level nearly all public conversation operates at — including, until this primer, every page in this section. It establishes the stakes and prescribes nothing. It can alarm you. It cannot tell you what to do.

2
Cost-benefit analysis

Costs and benefits both converted into dollars. Does the intervention return more money than it consumes? Useful in some settings, but it requires assigning a dollar value to health itself — and ultimately to a human life. That is a deeply uncomfortable exercise, which is one reason it is used less in health care than people assume.

3
Cost-effectiveness and cost-utility

Cost per unit of health gained. Not "does it pay for itself" but "what does it cost to produce one more year of healthy life?" This is the level where real decisions get made, and it is the dominant method in health economics worldwide. It is also the level almost nobody outside the field talks about — and the one that could change this conversation entirely.

What a QALY is

The third level needs a common unit of health, and the one the field settled on is the quality-adjusted life year, or QALY. One QALY is one year of life in full health. A year lived at roughly half of full function counts as about half a QALY. It is an attempt to capture, in a single number, both how long someone lives and how well.

Divide the extra cost of an intervention by the extra QALYs it produces, and you get an incremental cost-effectiveness ratio — the cost per QALY gained. It is a genuinely useful instrument. It lets you compare a cancer drug against a hip replacement against a screening program, in a common currency of health, and ask the plain question: what are we getting for the money?

A number worth knowing the history of

For decades, US analyses anchored on $50,000 per QALY as the benchmark of what a year of healthy life was worth paying for. That figure had little theoretical or empirical foundation — it traces back to the cost of dialysis decades ago and simply persisted. It has since drifted upward toward $100,000 and $150,000. The lesson is not that the method is worthless. It is that the number carries more authority than its origins deserve, and it should be read as a tool for thinking, not a verdict handed down.

Where the instrument is aimed

Here is where this stops being a methods lesson and becomes the case this primer exists to make. The cost-per-QALY instrument works. It is applied constantly. Look at what it is applied to:

$5,556

Every one of these is a discrete, packageable intervention inserted into primary care. A test. A device. A screening protocol. A monitoring technology. The instrument is pointed, almost without exception, at things that arrive as products.

What it is almost never pointed at is the thing this entire site is about: the delivery architecture itself. The continuity. The relationship. The arc of care held over time. Nobody is running the ratio on a person having a real primary care relationship versus a person cycling through episodic encounters, and asking what a year of healthy life costs under each.

The experiment that was run, and what it proved

One large-scale attempt to improve primary care was put through this analysis, and the result deserves far more attention than it received.

The United Kingdom's Quality and Outcomes Framework was the largest primary-care pay-for-performance programme in the world — an enormous, well-funded effort to improve care by attaching financial incentives to measured targets. When researchers modelled its cost-effectiveness, it came out at roughly £49,362 per QALY: well above the threshold the UK uses to decide what is worth funding. The published conclusion was blunt — continuing the programme was not cost-effective, and the country should either redesign it or pursue something else.

What that result actually tells us

This is not evidence that primary care fails the test. It is evidence that bolting incentives onto an unchanged delivery structure fails the test. The QOF did not rebuild how care was delivered. It layered measurement and payment on top of the existing architecture and hoped behaviour would follow. It is the most expensive demonstration available that you cannot buy your way to better delivery without changing the delivery. That is this framework's core case, tested at national scale, at a cost of billions — and confirmed.

The blind spot

Now the deeper problem, and the reason this primer matters more than any single figure in this section.

QALYs get assigned to interventions. A drug is credited with the health gain measured in its trial. A screening protocol is credited with the cancers it catches. But consider what had to happen for that drug to produce its QALYs: someone had to notice the problem early enough, get the patient to the right care at the right moment, and have them arrive in a condition where the drug could actually work. The relationship that did all of that is credited with nothing. It generates no QALYs of its own. It does not appear in the ratio.

The delivery system is invisible to the very instrument built to measure health value. The arc produces the health; the product takes the score.

This is not a small accounting quirk. It is the reason horizon-chasing keeps winning. When the scoreboard only counts things that arrive as products, the money flows to products — to the next drug, the next device, the next algorithm — while the unglamorous work of actually reaching people continuously goes unmeasured, and therefore unfunded, and therefore undone. The execution gap this framework has named from the beginning turns out to be built into the measuring instrument itself.

What the QALY gets wrong on its own terms

The method deserves one more honest criticism, because it bears directly on how this framework thinks about health.

By construction, a year lived with a disability scores below a full QALY. The arithmetic therefore treats a year of a disabled person's life as worth less than a year of an able-bodied person's — a conclusion most people reject the moment it is stated plainly. The concern is serious enough that the use of QALY thresholds in US federal coverage decisions has been legally constrained.

This framework's answer is built into its language. Capacity, as used throughout this site, is measured against the individual's own potential — not against an able-bodied ideal. Every person has a version of full capacity, and the clinical target is reaching theirs. That is not a softer standard. It is a more accurate one, and it repairs precisely the flaw the QALY carries.

What can honestly be said, and what would settle it

We will not give you a cost-per-QALY figure for this model. We do not have outcome data in a form clean enough to publish, and manufacturing a number to win a case would betray everything this site is built on. Anyone who hands you a tidy ICER for a delivery model should be asked, very carefully, where it came from.

What we have instead is fifteen years of practice, and a set of clinical observations that the instrument — pointed as it currently is — cannot see:

What the arc produces that nothing is counting
  • Patients told their ceiling had been reached who kept recovering, for years, past the point where the standard path stopped looking.
  • Conditions intercepted in their silent window — the prediabetic stretch, the drifting blood pressure — where the cheapest possible intervention is also the most effective, and where episodic care simply is not present to catch them.
  • Function restored rather than symptoms suppressed, so the same condition is not paid for again every month, indefinitely.
  • Patients who moved from dependence to genuine independence and stopped consuming care — not because they were discharged, but because they no longer needed it.

These are clinical observations, not evidence. They are the kind of observation that generates a hypothesis worth testing — and the test is entirely available. It requires a cohort in continuous, arc-oriented primary care compared against a matched cohort in standard episodic care; quality of life measured over time rather than events tallied; and a horizon long enough for the arc to actually complete, which for chronic conditions means years, not a quarter.

And here is the honest expectation, stated in advance: this model may well cost more per person per year, at least at first. The case has never been that good care is cheap. The case is that the health it produces per dollar is not currently being counted at all — and until someone counts it, the comparison that would settle this cannot even be made.

The framework's reading

Cost-of-illness data can only alarm. Cost per year of healthy life can justify. As long as the delivery system remains invisible to the instrument, every argument for building better primary care will be an argument from principle — and principle loses to a spreadsheet, every time. The path forward is not to abandon the measurement. It is to point it at the right thing. Aim the instrument at the arc, at continuity, at the relationship, and let the number fall where it falls. This framework is willing to be measured. It is asking to be. What it will not do is pretend the measuring has already been done.
