Cost of Care · The Landscape

The total

Before any breakdown, the numbers as a whole. American healthcare is a $5.3 trillion enterprise consuming nearly a fifth of the entire economy — and the fifty-year trajectory that produced that number is the argument this whole section rests on.

Total US national health expenditure in 2024 (CMS)
18.0%

The number, in plain terms.

In 2024, the United States spent $5.3 trillion on health care, a figure so large it resists comprehension. It is more than the entire economic output of all but a handful of countries on Earth. Averaged across the population, it comes to $15,474 for every person in the country, every year. And it accounts for 18.0% of gross domestic product, meaning close to one out of every five dollars moving through the American economy is spent on health care.

Spending grew 7.2% in 2024, similar to the 7.4% growth the year before — and notably, that growth was driven not by rising prices but by increased use and intensity of care. The two years 2023 and 2024 were the strongest consecutive years of health-spending growth since the early 1990s.

The trajectory, the trend we cannot ignore.

A single year's number, however large, is less revealing than the trend. Health spending has not simply grown — it has grown faster than almost everything else, for half a century.

$74B → $5.3T
Total health spending, 1970 to 2024 — a more than seventyfold rise in nominal dollars
5% → 18%
Health spending as a share of GDP, 1960 to 2024 — from 1 dollar in 20 to nearly 1 in 5
$2,208 → $15,474
Per-person spending, 1970 to 2024, in constant inflation-adjusted dollars — a sevenfold real increase

That last figure is the one to sit with. Adjusted for inflation — stripping out the effect of the general rise in prices — real spending per person still rose sevenfold in five decades. This is not the story of money simply being worth less over time. It is the story of a genuine, sustained, compounding increase in how much of a person's resources go to health care. Health spending has consistently outpaced the growth of the overall economy, decade after decade.

Where it is heading

The trajectory is not projected to bend on its own. Federal actuaries expect national health spending to reach roughly $8.6 trillion — about $24,200 per person — by 2033, climbing to more than 20% of GDP. One dollar in five becomes one dollar in five and rising. On the current path, health care's claim on the economy continues to grow.

What the number does not reveal.

Here is the fact that turns this from an accounting exercise into an argument: the United States spends more on health care than any nation in the history of the world, and does not lead the world in health. It spends roughly twice the per-capita average of comparable wealthy nations while ranking near the bottom of that group in life expectancy. The money is not buying the outcomes its size would predict.

That gap — between what is spent and what is received — is the subject of this entire section. The total is the headline. Every chapter that follows asks the harder question underneath it: if this much money is not the problem, and clearly is not the solution, then what is actually going on? Where does the money go, who pays it, how much is wasted, and why does a country that spends this much still fall short?

The framework's reading

This framework's answer runs through everything on this site: the deficit was never money, and never knowledge. It is delivery — the organized capacity to bring good care to real people continuously, before crisis. A system can spend $5.3 trillion and still fail at that, because spending and delivering are not the same thing. The size of the total is not evidence of a well-funded system doing its best against hard problems. It is evidence of how much a delivery failure can cost.
